Dataset and Variables

Variable Type Description
hh_id ID Household identifier
region Categorical (2 levels) North, South
resilience_score Continuous (0–100) Composite resilience index (higher = more resilient)
food_insecure Binary (0/1) 1 = moderately/severely food insecure (HFIAS-based)
num_meals_per_day Continuous Average meals per day (adult equivalent)
agro_diversity Integer (0–10) Number of different crops/livestock kept
distance_water_km Continuous Distance to main water source (km)
training_attended Binary (0/1) 1 = attended resilience training last year

The statistics in the above results can be extracted and presented in a table as follows.

vars <- c("training_attended", "region")

chi_results <- map_dfr(vars, function(v) {
  
  test <- chisq.test(table(dframe$food_insecure, dframe[[v]]))
  
  tibble(
    variable = v,
    chi_stat = unname(test$statistic),
    df       = unname(test$parameter),
    p_value  = test$p.value
  )
}) %>%
  mutate(
    chi_stat = round(chi_stat, 2),
    p_value = if_else(
      p_value < 0.001, "< .001", sprintf("%.3f", p_value)))

names(chi_results) <- c("Variable", "Chi-stat", "DF", "P-value")

labels = c(
  var_label(dframe[[vars[1]]]), 
  var_label(dframe[[vars[2]]]))

chi_results %>%
  mutate(Variable = labels) %>%

    align = "right",
    columns = `P-value`) %>%
  tab_options(

This analysis examined key determinants of household resilience and food insecurity using a dataset of 1,000 households across two regions (North and South). The study combined descriptive statistics, inferential tests, and regression modeling to identify the most important drivers of resilience and food insecurity.

  • Approximately 31% of households are food insecure, indicating a substantial vulnerability within the population.
  • Food insecurity is more prevalent in the South (30%) compared to the North (27%), highlighting regional disparities.

  • Households that attended resilience training show slightly lower food insecurity (29%) compared to non-attendees (32%).
  • However, statistical tests (Chi-square and logistic regression) show no significant relationship, suggesting that training alone may not be sufficient to reduce food insecurity.

  • Linear regression results show that: - Number of meals per day is the strongest predictor of resilience → Each additional meal increases resilience by ~5.7 points (p < 0.001) - Region is also significant
    → Households in the South have ~8 points lower resilience
  • Other factors (training, agro-diversity, distance to water) were not statistically significant
  • The model explains ~13.7% of variation in resilience, indicating that additional factors (e.g., income, assets) likely play a role

8.1.4 Determinants of Food Insecurity

  • Logistic regression identifies resilience score as the strongest predictor: - Each unit increase in resilience reduces odds of food insecurity by ~5% (p < 0.001)
  • Distance to water shows a positive but weak relationship: - Each additional kilometer increases odds of food insecurity by ~6% (not statistically significant)
  • Training, agro-diversity, and region show no significant effects

8.1.5 Relationships Between Key Variables

  • There is no significant correlation between resilience and agro-diversity
  • A significant relationship exists between distance to water and number of meals, suggesting water access may indirectly affect food consumption

8.1.6 Impact of Training

  • Independent t-test results suggest that training is associated with higher resilience scores
  • However, this effect is not consistent across models, indicating possible confounding factors

8.2 Overall Conclusions

  • Food access (meals per day) and resilience capacity are the most critical drivers of household well-being
  • Resilience plays a central protective role, significantly reducing the likelihood of food insecurity
  • Regional disparities persist, with the South consistently worse off
  • Training programs alone show limited impact, suggesting a need for more comprehensive or better-targeted interventions

8.2.1 Policy Implications

  • Prioritize interventions that improve household food access
  • Strengthen programs aimed at building resilience capacity
  • Address regional inequalities, particularly in the South
  • Reassess and enhance training program effectiveness
  • Improve access to water, which may indirectly influence food security

While the models are statistically significant, their explanatory power is modest, indicating that food insecurity and resilience are complex, multi-dimensional issues requiring broader socio-economic considerations.

9 Executive Summary (Pragraph)

This analysis examined the determinants of household resilience and food insecurity using data from 1,000 households across the North and South regions. Overall, 31% of households were found to be food insecure, indicating a significant level of vulnerability. Regional differences were observed, with food insecurity slightly higher in the South (30%) compared to the North (27%). While households that attended resilience training exhibited marginally lower food insecurity (29% vs 32%), statistical tests showed that this difference was not significant, suggesting that training alone may not be sufficient to improve food security outcomes.

Linear regression results identified number of meals per day as the strongest predictor of resilience, with each additional meal associated with an increase of approximately 5.7 points in resilience score. Region was also significant, with households in the South having resilience scores about 8 points lower than those in the North. Other factors, including agro-diversity, distance to water, and training attendance, were not statistically significant predictors. The model explained approximately 13.7% of the variation in resilience, indicating that additional factors not captured in the dataset likely influence resilience.

Logistic regression analysis showed that resilience score is the most important determinant of food insecurity, with each unit increase in resilience reducing the odds of food insecurity by about 5%. Distance to water had a positive but weak association with food insecurity, while training, agro-diversity, and region were not significant predictors. Overall, the findings highlight the central role of food access and resilience in shaping household well-being, while also pointing to persistent regional disparities and the need for more comprehensive interventions beyond training alone.
